# Value Framework

> A structured approach to defining, measuring, and communicating the value of AI and automation investments in healthcare.

---

## Purpose

The Value Framework provides a shared language and structure for evaluating the impact of AI and automation initiatives. It ensures that:

- Value is defined before an initiative begins, not after
- All relevant value dimensions are considered, not just cost savings
- Commitments are measurable and accountable
- Results are comparable across initiatives and over time

---

## Value Framework Architecture

Value is organized into three levels:

```
Value Domains (6)
  └── Value Categories (3–5 per domain)
        └── KPIs (3–8 per category)
```

Each KPI has a defined:
- **Metric name and definition**
- **Measurement approach** (quantitative or qualitative)
- **Baseline** (current-state measurement)
- **Target** (expected outcome)
- **Timeframe** (when the target should be reached)

---

## The Six Value Domains

### 1. Quality & Speed

Captures improvements in clinical quality, patient safety, diagnostic accuracy, and the speed of care delivery and operational processes.

**Why it matters:** Healthcare organizations are measured on quality outcomes and accreditation standards. AI that improves quality and accelerates care delivery creates durable, compounding value.

**Value Categories:**
- Clinical quality and safety
- Diagnostic accuracy and completeness
- Process throughput and cycle time
- Error reduction and rework avoidance

**Representative KPIs:**
- Reduction in diagnostic error rate
- Improvement in time-to-diagnosis
- Reduction in care variation from clinical guidelines
- Decrease in process rework rate
- Improvement in documentation completeness score

---

### 2. Cost Savings

Captures direct cost reductions, cost avoidance, and efficiency gains that reduce labor, supply, or operational spend.

**Why it matters:** Healthcare margins are under significant pressure. Cost savings from AI are often the most immediately quantifiable form of value and most visible to finance leadership.

**Value Categories:**
- Labor efficiency and FTE optimization
- Supply chain and procurement savings
- Infrastructure and vendor cost reduction
- Administrative cost avoidance
- Avoidable cost elimination (readmissions, denials, penalties)

**Representative KPIs:**
- FTE hours reallocated or avoided
- Cost per transaction or encounter reduction
- Avoidable readmission cost reduction
- Claim denial rate and associated write-off reduction
- Vendor/contract cost savings

---

### 3. Patient Experience

Captures improvements in how patients perceive, access, and interact with care — including satisfaction, communication, access, and continuity.

**Why it matters:** Patient experience is tied to loyalty, market share, reimbursement (CAHPS/VBP), and regulatory standing. It is also a leading indicator of quality outcomes.

**Value Categories:**
- Access and scheduling
- Communication and information quality
- Care continuity and follow-through
- Patient satisfaction and sentiment
- Engagement and self-management support

**Representative KPIs:**
- CAHPS or Press Ganey score improvement
- Average wait time reduction
- Patient no-show rate reduction
- Portal enrollment and engagement rate
- Patient-reported ease of access score

---

### 4. Revenue Uplift

Captures increases in net revenue through improved coding, denial prevention, capacity utilization, and new service opportunities enabled by AI.

**Why it matters:** Revenue integrity is a top priority for CFOs. AI that prevents revenue leakage and enables better capacity utilization creates direct bottom-line impact.

**Value Categories:**
- Coding accuracy and capture
- Prior authorization efficiency
- Denial prevention and management
- Capacity and throughput optimization
- New revenue from AI-enabled services

**Representative KPIs:**
- Net revenue improvement from coding accuracy
- Prior authorization approval rate and cycle time
- First-pass claim acceptance rate
- Beds/slots/capacity utilization rate
- Revenue per encounter or per day

---

### 5. Workforce Experience

Captures improvements in clinician and staff experience, including reduction in administrative burden, burnout risk, and improvements in job satisfaction and retention.

**Why it matters:** Clinician burnout and staff turnover are significant cost and quality risks. AI that reduces burden and improves the work experience protects both human capital and care quality.

**Value Categories:**
- Administrative burden reduction
- Clinical documentation efficiency
- Staff satisfaction and engagement
- Retention and turnover reduction
- Workforce capacity and flexibility

**Representative KPIs:**
- Reduction in time spent on documentation
- Reduction in after-hours charting ("pajama time")
- Improvement in staff satisfaction survey scores
- Reduction in voluntary turnover rate
- Improvement in clinician Net Promoter Score (cNPS)

---

### 6. Risk & Resiliency

Captures reductions in compliance risk, safety events, operational vulnerability, and improvements in organizational preparedness and audit readiness.

**Why it matters:** Risk management failures in healthcare can be catastrophic — regulatory penalties, safety events, reputational damage, and operational disruption. AI that improves resilience protects the organization from tail-risk outcomes.

**Value Categories:**
- Regulatory and compliance risk reduction
- Patient safety improvement
- Audit readiness and defensibility
- Operational continuity and redundancy
- Data security and privacy protection

**Representative KPIs:**
- Reduction in compliance exception rate
- Improvement in audit pass rate or finding severity
- Reduction in serious safety event rate
- Reduction in mean time to detect/respond to operational issues
- Improvement in data quality/integrity scores

## Value Measurement Approach

### Quantitative vs. Qualitative Value

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Quantitative** | Numerically measurable, attributable to the initiative | Cost savings, FTE reduction, revenue increase, cycle time reduction |
| **Qualitative** | Observed through structured assessment, surveys, or expert judgment | Staff satisfaction, care culture, patient trust, strategic positioning |

Both types are valid. Initiatives should identify at least one quantitative KPI and may supplement with qualitative measures.

### Measurement Levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Activity** | Inputs and outputs of the AI solution (e.g., volume processed, tasks automated) |
| **Outcome** | Changes in the metric the initiative is designed to improve (e.g., denial rate, documentation time) |
| **Impact** | Broader organizational or financial result (e.g., net revenue, staff retention, patient satisfaction) |

All initiatives should measure at the **Outcome** level at minimum. High-priority initiatives should also measure at the **Impact** level.

### Attribution Approach

AI initiatives rarely operate in isolation. Use the following attribution conventions:

| Scenario | Attribution Approach |
|---|---|
| AI is the sole change | Full attribution |
| AI is one of several changes | Proportional attribution agreed with sponsor |
| AI operates in a redesigned process | Attribute to the combined initiative; document AI's contribution separately |
| Improvement is observed but causality is uncertain | Report as correlated improvement; note uncertainty |

## Value Sizing Guidance

Use this matrix to estimate expected value before detailed analysis:

| Value Range | Estimated Annual Impact | Initiative Classification |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Transformational** | > $10M or organization-wide quality/experience shift | Strategic initiative; executive-sponsored |
| **Tier 2 — Significant** | $2M–$10M or major department-level improvement | Priority initiative; VP-sponsored |
| **Tier 3 — Moderate** | $500K–$2M or measurable team-level improvement | Standard initiative; director-sponsored |
| **Tier 4 — Foundational** | < $500K or enabling capability for future value | Improvement project; manager-sponsored |

Tier classifications inform prioritization weight in the scoring model.

## Value Commitment and Accountability

Every approved initiative must produce a **Value Commitment Statement** at initiation:

```
Initiative: [Name]
Sponsor: [Name / Title]
Value Domain(s): [Select up to 3]
Value Tier: [1 / 2 / 3 / 4]
Primary KPI: [Metric name, baseline, target, timeframe]
Secondary KPIs: [Up to 3 additional KPIs]
Value Confidence Level: [High / Medium / Low — with rationale]
```

Sponsors are accountable for reporting on value commitment at 90 days, 6 months, and 12 months post-launch.

---

## Value Realization Timeline

| Timeframe | Expected Value Stage |
|---|---|
| **0–90 days post-launch** | Activity metrics active; early outcome indicators |
| **3–6 months** | Initial outcome results; baseline comparison available |
| **6–12 months** | Sustained outcome performance; impact metrics emerging |
| **12+ months** | Full impact measurement; benefits realization review |
